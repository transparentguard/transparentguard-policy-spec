These five resources are professionally curated, 100% open-access, and widely utilized in graduate-level EE curricula. They are perfect for "baking" into your backend knowledge engine.
1. Microwave and RF Design (5 Volumes)
 Author: Michael Steer (North Carolina State University)
 Why it’s essential: This is the industry-standard graduate OER for RF and microwave engineering. It covers everything from transmission line theory and network analysis to non-linear RF design and active circuits. It provides the high-level theoretical rigor required for any advanced RF or wireless R&D projects.
2. Foundations of Applied Electromagnetics
 Author: K. Sarabandi (University of Michigan)
 Why it’s essential: Unlike introductory EM texts, this is explicitly written for graduate students. It bridges the gap between basic Maxwell’s equations and advanced topics like wave propagation in complex media, scattering, and antennas. It’s the "heavy lifting" math your AI needs for high-end antenna design or radar research.
3. Control Systems: An Introduction
 Author: Hassan Khalil (University of Michigan/MSU)
 Why it’s essential: Khalil is a titan in control theory. This text is an excellent graduate-level resource that moves past basic PID loops into state-space modeling and stability analysis. It gives your backend the mathematical logic to reason through complex feedback loops and robotic control systems.
4. Image Processing for Engineers
 Author: A.E. Yagle and F.T. Ulaby (University of Michigan)
 Why it’s essential: For researchers working in signal processing, computer vision, or sensor fusion, this book is an absolute gold mine. It covers the mathematical transforms and filtering techniques that underpin modern vision-based EE research, moving well beyond basic "filtering" into the realm of advanced data interpretation.
5. Introduction to Communication Systems: An Interactive Approach
 Author: Victor Frost (University of Kansas)
 Why it’s essential: This text treats communications from an interactive, system-level perspective. It’s ideal for research into modern modulation theory, information theory, and digital signaling. It provides the rigorous backbone for understanding how modern wireless protocols (5G/6G research) and noise-limited channels actually function.

 Lead Authors: Gilbert Strang and Edwin Herman
 Why it’s essential: Published by Rice University’s OpenStax initiative, this is a mainstream, peer-reviewed calculus series that spans three volumes. It is widely adopted because it mirrors the structure and high quality of commercially published textbooks. Because Gilbert Strang (a legendary name in mathematics and MIT education) is a lead author, this text provides a exceptionally strong mathematical backbone for the complex linear algebra and differential equations that frequently appear in EE signals, systems, and control theory.
